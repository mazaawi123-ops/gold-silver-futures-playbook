The Foundation

Core Principles

A risk-first operating system for trading gold (GC) and silver (SI) futures. The edge is not a secret indicator — it is sizing, discipline, and matching tactics to the market regime.

1

Risk before reward

Define your stop and exact dollar risk before entry. Size from risk, never from conviction. The position size is an output of the stop, not a feeling.

2

Trade the regime, not the ticker

Decide first whether the market is trending, ranging, or squeezing — then apply the tactic that fits. Using a range tactic in a trend (or vice-versa) is how good setups become losers.

3

Demand asymmetry

Only take trades where the realistic reward is at least 2× the risk (more for swing/position). One 3R winner can pay for several small, disciplined losses.

4

Respect leverage

Futures margin is a performance bond, not a down payment. A small adverse move against an oversized position can wipe your margin before you react. Keep effective leverage modest.

5

Process over outcome

Judge yourself on whether you followed the plan, not whether the trade won. A disciplined loss is a good trade; a reckless win is a bad habit forming.

6

Plan around the calendar

CPI, NFP, and FOMC inject violent volatility into metals via the dollar and real yields. Reduce size or stand aside — do not get caught full-size into a print.

7

Limits are sacred

A daily max-loss and a max-trades cap end the session automatically. The market will be open tomorrow; a blown account will not.

8

Journal & review weekly

Screenshot entries and exits, log the reasoning, and review every week. Your journal is the only honest record of whether the process is working.

Targets & Routine

Goals & Daily To-Do

Concrete targets per style, plus a repeatable daily routine. Targets are aspirations to organize behavior — not promises of income.

The $50/day goal — an aspiration, not a guarantee

On a small account, $50/day is a useful focusing target, but it is also aggressive: on a $5,000 account it is ~1% per day, which is not sustainable as a guaranteed rate of return. Some days the highest-quality decision is no trade at all. Treat the target as a ceiling that lets you stop, not a quota you must force. Never widen a stop or add size to "make the number."

Day trades

Aim for $50/day. Close everything before the session ends. Stop trading the moment you hit the target — or the daily max loss, whichever comes first.

Swing trades

Aim for 2R+ per trade held over several days. No daily profit target — you are paid for catching the move, not for clocking in.

Position trades

Aim for 4R+ or trail a macro regime move over weeks. Use smaller risk and low / no leverage so wide noise cannot shake you out.

The leverage reality check

At 500× leverage, a roughly 0.2% adverse move (1 ÷ 500) can wipe out your entire posted margin — and that is before fees, spread, and maintenance-margin requirements, which trigger a margin call or liquidation even sooner. High leverage does not increase your edge; it only shortens the distance between you and zero.

Daily to-do

Pre-market Prepare

  • Scan macro & overnight news: DXY, real yields, Fed tone.
  • Mark key support / resistance on gold & silver.
  • Identify the regime: trending, ranging, or squeezing.
  • Note today's high-impact events and their times.
  • Set the day's risk budget and write down max loss.

In-session Execute

  • Only take setups that pass the full pre-trade checklist.
  • Size every trade with the position calculator.
  • Set the protective stop the instant you enter.
  • Log entry, stop, target, and reasoning as you go.
  • Stop at the +$50 target or the daily max loss.

Post-market Review

  • Screenshot each trade's chart with entry/exit marked.
  • Journal what went right and what broke the plan.
  • Tag any rule violations — fix the process, not the P&L.
  • Update tomorrow's levels and watchlist.
  • Close the platform; protect your headspace.
Your Instruments

Toolkit

A deliberately small set of tools. Each answers one question; together they describe trend, volatility, location, and the macro backdrop that moves metals.

Bollinger Bands (20, 2)

Volatility envelope and regime read. Tells you whether the market is trending (walking a band), ranging (bouncing between bands), or squeezing (bands pinched).

Moving averages (20 / 50 / 200)

Trend backbone and dynamic support/resistance. Stacked and sloping = trend; tangled and flat = chop. The 200 frames the bigger picture.

Structure & key levels

Horizontal support/resistance, prior highs/lows, and round numbers. Trades are taken at levels, not in the middle of nowhere.

Gold-silver ratio

Relative strength gauge (gold price ÷ silver price). Helps decide which metal to favor and confirms or questions a single-metal signal.

Macro drivers

The US dollar (DXY), real yields, and Fed rate expectations are the primary fundamental levers for metals. Know which way they're leaning before you trade.

Momentum & volume

RSI / momentum and volume confirmation. A break or reversal with conviction behind it beats a quiet drift through a level.

Economic calendar

CPI, NFP, FOMC, and major speeches. Know the times in your session timezone so volatility events never surprise you mid-trade.

Read It First

Market Regimes

Every chart is in one of three states. Tap a card to expand its signature, the Bollinger behavior, the tactic that fits, and the trap that catches traders using the wrong one.

Signature

Higher highs/lows (or lower highs/lows). Moving averages stacked and sloping. Bands sloping with price.

Bollinger behavior

Price "walks the band" — repeatedly tagging the upper (uptrend) or lower (downtrend) band. %B sits persistently above 0.8 or below 0.2.

Tactic

Trade with the trend. Buy pullbacks toward the middle band / 20-MA in an uptrend; sell rallies into it in a downtrend. Trail your stop.

The trap

Fading the band. A band tag in a trend is continuation, not a reversal — shorting strength here is how accounts bleed.

Signature

Flat, parallel bands. A flat middle band. Price rotating between horizontal support and resistance with no net progress.

Bollinger behavior

%B swings the full 0 → 1 and back. Touches of the upper and lower bands alternate as price reverts to the mean.

Tactic

Fade the edges back toward the mean: sell near the upper band / resistance, buy near the lower band / support — with confirmation and tight stops just beyond the band.

The trap

Every range eventually breaks. A band tag during a genuine breakout is a trap — respect the level that finally gives way.

Signature

Bandwidth contracts to multi-week lows. The bands pinch tight; candles shrink. The market is coiling.

Bollinger behavior

A "squeeze" — low bandwidth — forecasts an expansion in volatility. It signals timing, not direction.

Tactic

Don't predict. Wait for the expansion: a decisive close outside a band, ideally with volume. Trade the break and manage with the opposite band.

The trap

Fakeouts. The first push out of a squeeze often reverses. Demand confirmation and keep risk defined — anticipation gets punished here.

Indicator Reference

Bollinger Bands Reference

A volatility envelope around a moving average. The defaults are a 20-period simple moving average with bands two standard deviations away.

Formulas

ComponentFormula
Middle band20-period SMA of price
Upper bandSMA + (k × σ)
Lower bandSMA − (k × σ)
%B(price − lower) ÷ (upper − lower)
Bandwidth(upper − lower) ÷ middle

Defaults: period 20, multiplier k = 2, where σ is the standard deviation of price over the same 20 periods. A shorter period reacts faster but adds noise; a longer period is smoother but lags.

Reading %B and bandwidth

  • %B > 1 — price closed above the upper band (strength or exhaustion — context decides).
  • %B < 0 — price closed below the lower band.
  • %B ≈ 0.5 — price sits at the middle band / mean.
  • Low bandwidth = a squeeze: compression that forecasts an expansion in volatility.
  • High bandwidth = an already-extended, volatile market.

Caveats — bands are not signals

Bollinger Bands measure relative volatility; they are not standalone buy/sell triggers. A touch of a band is not by itself a signal — in a trend, price walks the band for a long time. A squeeze tells you a move is coming, not which way it goes. Always combine bands with trend, structure, momentum, and volume, and tune the settings to your timeframe through honest backtesting rather than curve-fitting to the last chart.

Avoid These

Common Mistakes

Most blown accounts die from the same short list. Read it often — recognizing the pattern in the moment is what saves the trade.

Oversizing / over-leverage

The single fastest way to ruin. Size from the stop, keep effective leverage low, and respect the calculator's number.

Trading full-size into events

Entering CPI / NFP / FOMC at full size invites slippage and gapped stops. Cut size or stand aside.

No predefined stop

"I'll watch it" is not a stop. Without a hard invalidation set before entry, a small loss becomes an account event.

Moving stops against the plan

Widening a stop to avoid being wrong converts a planned 1R loss into an unplanned disaster. Stops move to protect, never to hope.

Revenge trading

Chasing a loss with a bigger, unplanned trade. This is exactly what the daily max-loss limit exists to stop.

Band touch = reversal

Treating every Bollinger band tag as a turn. In a trend, price walks the band — fading it bleeds you out.

Ignoring the higher timeframe

Taking a counter-trend scalp against an obvious daily trend. Align with the bigger picture or pass.

No daily loss limit

Without a hard stop on the day, one bad session can erase weeks of progress. Set it, honor it.

Chasing extended moves

Entering after the move has already run — buying the top tick. Wait for the pullback to a level or pass entirely.

Overtrading

Forcing trades to "make the $50" or out of boredom. Three A-grade setups beat twenty mediocre ones.
